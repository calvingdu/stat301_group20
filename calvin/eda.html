Pre-selection of Variables

Some variables are highly correlated or redundant and we may remove. I grouped some of these variables above but here are some more concrete examples:

  • height and weight are represented by bodymassindexbmi

  • totalbodywatertbw, extracellularwaterecw, and intracellularwatericw are collinear measures of hydration

  • There's some scientific terms I don't really understand, but some prelimary factors showed that variables under binary comorbidity indicators (coronaryarterydiseasecad, diabetesmellitusdm, etc.) can be summed into a single index as they represent the same thing

Scientific Question

The question I'll aim for is:

We want to examine the association between a person's total body water (L) (response) and predictors related to lean mass, gender and body fat ratio and other proportional measurements.

Response variable: totalbodywatertbw

Explanatory variables: leanmasslm, totalbodyfatratiotbfr, gender, height, weight, bmi

Model type: Multiple Linear Regression

Focus: Inference to identify which body composition features contribute most to totalbodywatertbw

ggplot(df, aes(x = leanmasslm, y = totalbodywatertbw, color = gender)) +
  geom_point(alpha = 0.7) +
  geom_smooth(method = "lm", se = FALSE) +
  labs(
    title = "Total Body Water vs Lean Mass by Gender",
    x = "Lean Mass (kg)",
    y = "Total Body Water (L)",
    color = "Gender"
  )

`geom_smooth()` using formula = 'y ~ x'

Relevancy:

This plot is relevant as it let's us explore how much as someone get's more lean mass, how is this assocaited with how much water is in their body. We also want to see how this changes between gender

Results:

I originally thought that these would have positive relationships but interestingly, the total amount of water decreases with lean mass (negative relationship).

We can also see that for males, the slope is more significant meaning that for every additional kg of lean mass, there's less L of total water in the body compared to females.

What did we learn?

Overall, the plot indicates that lean mass and total body water are inversely associated, and gender shifts the overall TBW level upward for males relative to females and will also provide a more significant slope. This suggests there's an interaction term here we can look at. I'm also curious on the negative relationship since I thought it would be more mass -> bigger -> more water.
